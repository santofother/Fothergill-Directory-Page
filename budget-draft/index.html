How to play on this page

Everything you need to run a full salary-cap draft is built into the board above — there is nothing to install, no account to create, and no second person required. You begin by setting up your draft: name your franchise, choose one to three rival general managers to compete against, and set how sharp those AI GMs are. Every general manager at the table, including you, is handed the exact same $200 salary cap and the exact same empty ten-slot roster to fill.

The draft then runs as a blind-bid auction, one player at a time. The best available player is placed "on the block," and every GM who still needs a slot that player can fill submits a single secret bid at the same moment. You set your bid with the plus and minus buttons or the quick-bid chips, then press Place blind bid — or Pass if you'd rather save your money. Once you commit, every AI GM's sealed bid is revealed at once. The highest bid wins the player and pays exactly what they bid; a tie is broken in favour of whichever GM has more cap space left. The winner slots the player onto their roster, the cap is deducted, and the next player comes up. This continues until the board can no longer fill anyone's roster.

Because the bids are blind, the game is a test of nerve and reading the room. You can see every rival's remaining cap and which slots they still need — that public information is your biggest weapon. If you know a rival is desperate for a quarterback and has $80 left, you know roughly how high you'd have to go to beat them, and whether it's worth it. Overpay for one superstar and you'll be filling the back of your roster with the scraps nobody else wanted; hoard your cap too long and the players you needed will already be gone.

When the draft ends, every roster is scored by adding up the overall rating of every player on it. The highest total wins — not the team with the single best player, but the team that spent its cap most efficiently across all ten slots. You'll see a full breakdown of every franchise's roster, what each player cost, and where the draft was won and lost.

The roster you're building

Each franchise fills the same ten starting slots, drawn from a pool of famous players at every position. The slots are: one quarterback, one running back, two wide receivers, one tight end, one flex spot that accepts a running back, receiver, or tight end, and then four defenders — a defensive lineman, a linebacker, a cornerback, and a safety. The flex slot is where a lot of drafts are won: because it accepts three different positions, a savvy GM can wait for value to fall to them there rather than paying full price at a locked position.

Every player carries a single overall rating from the low 60s to a maximum of 99, generated from their real career accolades — Pro Bowls, All-Pro selections, MVP awards, and championships — blended with their attributes. That rating is both what you're chasing (it's what your roster is scored on) and the anchor for what a player is truly worth at auction. Learning to feel the gap between a 99 and an 88 at a position, and whether that gap is worth twenty dollars of cap, is the entire skill of the game.

The rules, step by step

  1. Everyone gets the same cap. All GMs start with $200 and an identical empty roster of ten slots.
  2. Players are auctioned one at a time. The highest-rated player still useful to someone is placed on the block.
  3. Bids are blind and simultaneous. Every GM who can use the player submits one secret bid, then all bids are revealed together.
  4. Highest bid wins and pays that amount. Ties go to the GM with more remaining cap. The player fills one of the winner's open slots — a dedicated position slot first, otherwise the flex.
  5. You must be able to finish your roster. The game never lets you bid so much that you couldn't afford at least $1 for every remaining empty slot, so you can always complete a legal team.
  6. The draft ends when rosters are full (or no remaining player fits anyone's open slots). Every roster is then scored by total overall rating.
  7. Highest total rating wins. If two franchises tie on rating, the one with more leftover cap wins the tiebreak.

Choosing GM difficulty

The rival GMs are not picking randomly — each one runs the same valuation engine you're playing against, but with a different amount of noise and aggression layered on top. The difficulty setting changes how accurately they price players and how disciplined they are with their cap.

Easy

Easy GMs value players loosely, with a lot of random noise in their bids. They'll frequently over-pay for a name they like and under-value a quiet star, leaving obvious bargains on the board for you to scoop up. A forgiving place to learn how the auction flows.

Medium

Medium GMs value players reasonably and spread their cap sensibly across the roster, but there's still enough randomness in their bids that a sharp reader can find edges. This is the fair fight for someone who knows the game.

Hard

Hard GMs price players tightly, stretch appropriately for elite talent, and rarely leave a clear bargain sitting on the board. Beating them means genuinely nailing player value and timing your aggression for the moments they can't match you.

Sharp

Sharp GMs are the benchmark: near-perfect valuations, disciplined cap management, and a willingness to pay a small premium to lock down the players that matter most. They will punish every overpay and every slot you leave to chance. Out-drafting a room of Sharp GMs is a real achievement.

Strategy: how to win

Value tiers, not names

Great GMs think in tiers, not individual players. If three receivers are rated within a couple of points of one another, there is no reason to pay a premium for the first one off the board — let a rival spend big, then take the near-identical option a tier down for far less. Knowing where the real drop-offs are between tiers is most of the game.

Budget for the endgame

The most common way to lose is to run out of usable cap with slots still to fill, forcing you to roster whoever is left for a dollar. Always keep enough powder dry to field a complete, respectable team. Depth across ten slots beats one superstar surrounded by minimum-bid filler almost every time.

Read the room's cap

Every rival's remaining cap and open slots are shown to you. That is public information you should be constantly using: a GM with no cap left can't outbid you, and a GM who still needs three positions with $40 left is about to get squeezed. Nominate and bid with those pressures in mind.

Exploit the flex

Because the flex slot accepts three positions, it is the easiest place to capture value. Rather than paying up for your second running back, you can let the board come to you and drop whichever of a runner, receiver, or tight end falls furthest below its rating into the flex.

The winner of a budget draft is rarely the GM with the best single player. It's the GM who spent efficiently across the whole roster while everyone else was busy overpaying at the top.

How the AI general managers decide

Each AI GM values every player from the same rating you see, converted into a market price on a curve where elite players cost exponentially more than average ones. On each auction, a GM first checks whether the player fills one of its open slots — if not, it passes outright. If the player is useful, the GM works out a target bid from three things: its fair share of cap per remaining slot, how far above baseline the player's rating sits (it will stretch well past fair share for a genuine star, and well below it for a role player), and a personality factor that makes some GMs naturally more aggressive than others. Difficulty then adds or removes random noise around that target, and the Sharp tier layers on a small premium to secure the players it wants most.

Crucially, every GM caps its bid so that it can always afford at least a dollar for each of its other empty slots — the same discipline the game enforces on you. That is why the AI rarely blows itself up early, and why beating the harder tiers comes down to reading which players they'll fight for and which they'll let slide, then bidding one dollar more than they will at exactly the right moment.

Frequently asked questions

Is this NFL Budget Draft game really free?

Yes. The complete single-player draft runs free in your browser with no account, no download, and no in-game purchases. The site is supported by lightweight advertising, explained in full on the privacy policy page.

Do I need to follow the NFL closely to play?

Deep knowledge is a real advantage — knowing which names are underrated is how you find bargains — but the overall ratings and accolades are shown on every player card, so a casual fan can absolutely play and improve.

Is this the same as fantasy football?

It's related but distinct. Instead of drafting in turn order, every GM competes for every player under a shared salary cap in a blind auction, which puts the focus squarely on valuation and bidding rather than draft position.

How is the winner decided?

When all rosters are full, each is scored by adding up the overall rating of every player on it. The highest total wins; a tie is broken by whoever has more leftover cap.

Can I play on my phone?

Yes. The board is responsive with large tap targets for bidding, and works on modern phones, tablets, and desktops.

What's the difference between this and the full version?

This page is a streamlined, self-contained single-player auction. The full version adds online multiplayer, a much larger player pool, head coaches, clue cards, and a play-by-play game simulator that actually plays your roster out on the field.

How to play on this page

NFL Budget Draft is three games braided together: an NFL trivia quiz, a salary-cap auction, and a game of social deduction. Everything you need runs right here in your browser — nothing to install, no account, and no second person required. You begin by naming your franchise, choosing one to three rival general managers, and setting how much football those AI GMs know. Every GM at the table, including you, is handed the same $200 salary cap and the same empty ten-slot roster to fill.

Here is the twist that makes the game: you never see who you're bidding on. Each player comes up as a mystery, revealed only through a short stack of trivia clues — things like "Won the Heisman Trophy at Oklahoma State," "Retired in his prime," or "Played his entire career with one AFC North franchise." From those hints you have to work out who it is, decide how good he actually is, and place a single blind bid using the plus and minus buttons or the quick-bid chips. Then you either commit the bid or pass.

Once you lock in, every AI GM's sealed bid is revealed at the same moment, the mystery player's name and rating are unveiled, and the highest bidder wins him for exactly what they bid. The player fills one of the winner's open slots, their cap is docked, and the next mystery comes up. This repeats until the rosters are full.

The skill is in the reading. A clue card for a legend is deliberately vague — the game strips the dead-giveaway facts from famous players, so "3× MVP" never appears and you have to recognise them. A lesser-known player, by contrast, usually gets one pinpoint clue that lets you crack him if you know your football. Recognise a genuinely good player that the room can't place, and you'll win him for a song. Fail to notice that the vague clues are actually describing an all-time great, and you'll let a bargain walk — or worse, overpay for a household name everyone else also recognised.

Why it's really a bluffing game

Because every bid is blind and simultaneous, the hard question isn't "how good is this player?" — it's "how much will everyone else bid?" Every rival's remaining cap and open slots are public, so you always know who can outspend you. What you don't know is who has identified the mystery player. That's the deduction layer.

If the clues are famous enough that everyone will obviously know him, you have to bid near his true value to win — and there's no bargain to be had. But if the clues are obscure and you're confident you're the only one at the table who recognises the name, you can win him for barely above what a clueless GM throws out as a shot in the dark. The leverage cuts both ways: bid too timidly on a player you know and a rival who also knows him snipes him for a dollar more; bid too aggressively on a player nobody wanted and you've wasted cap you'll need later. Reading how much your opponents know is the whole game.

The roster you're building

Each franchise fills the same ten starting slots: one quarterback, one running back, two wide receivers, one tight end, one flex spot that accepts a running back, receiver, or tight end, and then four defenders — a defensive lineman, a linebacker, a cornerback, and a safety. Each mystery player's position is shown (you always know whether you're bidding on a quarterback or a corner); only the identity is hidden. The flex slot is where value tends to fall, because it accepts three positions and lets you wait for a bargain instead of paying full freight at a locked spot.

When a player is finally revealed, he carries a single overall rating from the low 60s up to 99, built from his real career accolades — Pro Bowls, All-Pro nods, MVPs, and championships. That rating is what your roster is scored on at the end, and it's the true value you were trying to estimate from the clues while the identity was hidden.

The rules, step by step

  1. Everyone gets the same cap. All GMs start with $200 and an identical empty roster of ten slots.
  2. Players are auctioned one at a time as mysteries. You see the position and a few trivia clues — never the name or rating — until after the bidding.
  3. Bids are blind and simultaneous. Every GM who can use the player submits one secret bid, then all bids and the player's identity are revealed together.
  4. Highest bid wins and pays that amount. Ties go to the GM with more remaining cap. The player fills a dedicated slot first, otherwise the flex.
  5. You can always finish your roster. The game never lets you bid so much that you couldn't keep at least $1 for every remaining empty slot.
  6. The draft ends when rosters are full. Every roster is then scored by total overall rating.
  7. Highest total rating wins. A tie is broken by whoever has more leftover cap.

Choosing GM knowledge

The difficulty setting is really a measure of how much football your rival GMs know — how likely they are to correctly identify a mystery player from the clues, and how disciplined their bids are. The less they know, the more deep-cut bargains slip through for you to grab; the more they know, the harder it is to hide a star from the room.

Casual

Casual GMs only recognise the true household names. Deep cuts and cleverly-clued veterans sail right past them, so a fan who knows their football can steal bargains all draft long. A forgiving place to learn.

Fan

Fan GMs recognise most well-known players but still miss the deeper cuts, and their bids carry enough noise that a sharp reader can find edges. The fair fight for someone who follows the league.

Diehard

Diehard GMs crack most clue cards, price players tightly, and rarely let an obvious bargain sit. Beating them means genuinely knowing the middle and back of the player pool, not just the stars.

Historian

Historian GMs recognise nearly everyone, from all-time legends to obscure role players, and bid with real discipline. Hiding a player from them is almost impossible — you win by valuation and nerve, not by out-trivia-ing the room.

Strategy: how to win

Hunt the deep cuts

The bargains are players who are genuinely good but not famous — the ones whose clue cards the weaker GMs won't crack. If you can identify a quiet star, you can win him for a fraction of his rating because nobody else at the table knows to bid.

Don't overpay for the obvious

When the clues point unmistakably at a legend, everyone sees it, and the bidding will run to his true value. There's no edge there — pay up only if you truly need the slot, and look to make your money on the mysteries instead.

Bid one dollar more than you think they will

Against a player you've identified, the winning bid is exactly one more than the highest rival who also identified him. Read the room: how famous are these clues, how much cap do your rivals have, and how badly do they need this position? Then bid to beat them by the smallest margin that still wins.

Budget for the endgame

The most common way to lose is to run out of usable cap with slots still to fill, forcing you to roster whoever's left for a dollar. Keep enough powder dry to field a full, respectable team — depth across ten slots beats one superstar surrounded by filler.

The winner isn't the GM with the best single player. It's the GM who knew which mysteries were worth chasing, and read exactly how much the room would pay for each one.

How the AI general managers decide

Each AI GM plays the same puzzle you do. When a mystery player comes up, the GM first rolls to see whether it recognises him — a chance driven by how famous the player is and how much football that difficulty tier knows. Famous players are identified by almost everyone; obscure ones only by the sharpest GMs. If a GM recognises the player, it bids toward his true value, stretched for quality and scaled by its own cap and roster needs. If it doesn't recognise him, it can only judge the surface of the clue card, so it bids cautiously and largely blind to how good he really is — which is exactly why a player you know but the room doesn't can be stolen so cheaply.

Every GM also caps its bid so it can always keep a dollar for each remaining slot, the same discipline the game enforces on you. On the reveal you'll see not just who bid what, but which GMs actually recognised the player — the clearest window into whether a bargain was there to be had.

Frequently asked questions

Is this NFL Budget Draft game really free?

Yes. The complete single-player game runs free in your browser with no account, no download, and no in-game purchases. The site is supported by lightweight advertising, explained in full on the privacy policy page.

Do I need to know NFL history to play?

The more football you know, the bigger your edge — recognising players from clues is the whole skill. But every clue card shows the player's position and accolade-based hints, and the player is revealed after each auction, so you'll learn as you go.

Why can't I see who I'm bidding on?

That's the point of the game. The player is a mystery revealed only through trivia clues; identifying him from the hints — and judging whether your opponents can too — is how you find bargains and avoid overpaying.

How is the winner decided?

When all rosters are full, each is scored by adding up the overall rating of every player on it. The highest total wins; a tie is broken by whoever has more leftover cap.

Can I play on my phone?

Yes. The board is responsive with large tap targets for bidding, and works on modern phones, tablets, and desktops.

What's the difference between this and the full version?

This page is a streamlined, self-contained single-player game. The full version adds online multiplayer, a much larger player pool, head coaches, richer clue cards, and a play-by-play game simulator that actually plays your roster out on the field.
